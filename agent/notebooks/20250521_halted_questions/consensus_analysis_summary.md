### Analysis

1. **Types of Questions with High Consensus**: Questions with perfect agreement (especially those where all models answered correctly) tend to fall into several categories:
   - Factual questions with clear, well-established answers (e.g., Q68: "Which Of The Following Planets Has The Coldest Surfaces Temperatures?")
   - Questions about global statistics that are widely reported (e.g., Q4: "What Share Of The World's Population Is Undernourished?")
   - Questions about climate science fundamentals (e.g., Q67: "Which Of The Following Gases Does Not Contribute To Global Warming?")
   - Questions about basic economic trends (e.g., Q49: "Did A Majority Of The World's Population Get Richer Or Poorer In The Last 30 Years?")
   - Questions with clear definitions and established metrics (e.g., Q36: "What Is The Global Unemployment Rate?")

2. **Questions Where Models Consistently Get Wrong Answers**: The questions where models consistently provided incorrect answers (wrong or very_wrong) share these characteristics:
   - Questions requiring nuanced understanding of regional differences (e.g., Q14: "In Which Region Will You Find The Largest Share Of People With Cardiovascular Disease?")
   - Questions about complex causal relationships (e.g., Q10: "What's The Main Reason Why Overweight Is More Common In Richer Countries?")
   - Questions involving specific numerical estimates that may not be commonly reported (e.g., Q59: "What Share Of The Global Population Have Already Experienced A Temperature Increase Of 1.5 Degrees Celsius In At Least One Season?")
   - Questions about economic comparisons between regions (e.g., Q26: "The Size Of The Economy Of Sub-Saharan Africa Is About The Same As The Size Of The Economy As:")
   - Questions requiring up-to-date information on changing trends (e.g., Q62: "Between 2000 And 2005, On Average 383 Natural Disasters Were Registered Each Year In The World. What Was The Number During The Past 5 Years?")

3. **Impact of Confidence Threshold**: The distribution of consensus levels reveals important insights about confidence thresholds:
   - At 100% consensus threshold, we capture 35 questions (36% of total), with 25 correct answers and 10 indecisive ones, but no wrong answers
   - Lowering the threshold to 80% increases coverage to 69 questions (70% of total), but introduces 5 consistently wrong answers
   - This suggests that a high confidence threshold (requiring perfect agreement) effectively filters out incorrect answers but at the cost of coverage
   - A more balanced approach might be to use 100% consensus for high-stakes applications where precision is critical, while using 80% consensus for broader coverage in less critical contexts
   - The presence of indecisive answers even at high consensus levels suggests models are effectively calibrated to express uncertainty when appropriate

4. **Indecisive Answer Analysis**: The 40 questions where models were frequently indecisive reveal important patterns:
   - Questions requiring very specific numerical data that might not be widely available (e.g., Q77: "What Share Of Forests In The World Are Managed With A Long-Term Management Plan?")
   - Questions about regional statistics in developing areas where data collection may be less robust (e.g., Q38: "What Share Of The African Population Can Be Classified As Middle Class?")
   - Questions involving predictions or future trends (e.g., Q39: "Of All Jobs Worldwide, How Many Can Probably Be Completely Automated Within 30 Years?")
   - Questions with potentially ambiguous terms or definitions (e.g., Q73: "What Share Of The World's Oceans Suffer From Poor Waste Management, Overfishing And Bad Fishing Practices?")
   - Questions where the answer might have changed significantly in recent years (e.g., Q95: "In 1990, There Were 4 Countries Where Populists Were In Power. In How Many Countries Are Populists In Power Today?")
   - This suggests models are appropriately calibrated to express uncertainty when faced with questions requiring specialized knowledge or where data might be unreliable

5. **Implications**: These findings have several important implications for LLM deployment and evaluation:
   - **Consensus as a Quality Signal**: High model consensus (especially 100% agreement) strongly correlates with correct answers, suggesting multi-model consensus could be used as a quality control mechanism
   - **Complementary Strengths**: The variation in answers for low consensus questions suggests different models have complementary knowledge, supporting ensemble approaches
   - **Knowledge Gaps**: Consistent errors or indecisiveness across models highlight common knowledge gaps that should be addressed in future training
   - **Confidence Calibration**: Models appear well-calibrated in expressing uncertainty for questions with ambiguous or less available information
   - **Evaluation Strategy**: For factual question-answering systems, focusing evaluation on questions with low model consensus would be more efficient at finding potential issues
   - **User Experience Design**: Systems could present answers differently based on consensus levels - high consensus answers could be presented with greater confidence, while low consensus answers could be presented with appropriate caveats or alternative viewpoints
